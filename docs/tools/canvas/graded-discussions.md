# Creating a Graded Discussion

Graded discussions combine the engagement of a discussion board with accountability through a grade. They're excellent for assessing critical thinking, participation, and written communication.

**Time to complete:** ~5 minutes

---

## Creating a graded discussion

1. Go to **Discussions** in the course navigation.
2. Click **"+ Discussion"**.
3. Add a **title** and write your **discussion prompt** using the Rich Content Editor.
4. Check the box **"Graded"** — this unlocks grading options.
5. Set the **points**, **assignment group**, and **due date**.
6. Click **"Save & Publish"** (or Save to keep as draft).

---

## Discussion settings to consider

| Setting | What It Does | Recommendation |
|---------|--------------|----------------|
| **Allow Threaded Replies** | Students can reply to each other's posts | Turn on — creates real conversation |
| **Users Must Post Before Seeing Replies** | Students write their own response before seeing classmates' | Great for original thinking — prevents copying |
| **Allow Liking** | Students can "like" posts | Optional — adds engagement for older students |
| **Graded** | Adds the discussion to the gradebook | Turn on for accountability |
| **Group Discussion** | Each group has their own discussion thread | Use for small group activities |

!!! tip "Require posting first"
    The **"Must post before seeing replies"** option is a game-changer. It ensures every student develops their own ideas before being influenced by classmates. Highly recommended for higher-order thinking prompts.

---

## Grading discussions

Graded discussions appear in **SpeedGrader** just like assignments:

1. Open the discussion.
2. Click **"SpeedGrader"** (or access it from the Gradebook).
3. Read each student's posts and replies.
4. Assign a score and leave feedback.

!!! info "What counts as a 'submission'?"
    A student's discussion "submission" is their initial post. You'll see all their replies too, but the initial post triggers the submission in the gradebook.
